## Key Concepts
### 1. Design Thinking — The 5 Stages
Stage | What You Do
1. Empathise | Understand the people who have the problem. Do not assume — ask, observe, listen. Who is affected? How does this problem feel to them?
2. Define | Clearly state the problem. Use this format: "[Person] needs [need] because [insight]." Avoid jumping to solutions.
3. Ideate | Generate as many ideas as possible without judging any of them. Quantity over quality at this stage. Wild ideas welcome.
4. Prototype | Build the simplest possible version of your solution — fast and cheap. A sketch, a flowchart, a simple demo is fine.
5. Test | Get real feedback. Show your prototype to someone who has the problem. What works? What doesn't? Iterate.

### 2. Where Does AI Actually Help?
Not every problem needs AI. Here is a framework for deciding when AI adds value:
✅  AI is a good fit when...
The task involves pattern recognition in large amounts of data (e.g., identifying disease in thousands of images) / Personalisation at scale is needed (e.g., recommending different content to millions of users) / Speed matters more than occasional errors (e.g., spam filtering — some false positives are acceptable) / The task is repetitive and rule-based at its core, but with too many variations for manual rules / Natural language understanding or generation is central to the problem

⚠️  AI is NOT a good fit when...
The decision has life-or-death consequences and errors are unacceptable without human review / You have very little training data (AI needs lots of examples to work well) / The problem requires genuine common sense, novel reasoning, or emotional nuance / Transparency and explainability are legally or ethically required (e.g., credit decisions, medical diagnosis) / A simple rule-based system or spreadsheet would do the job just as well

### 3. Project Options for Days 8–9
Choose one of the following project tracks. Each is achievable in approximately 2 × 3 hours with the provided tools:
Project Track | Description
Track A: Smart Chatbot | Build a custom AI assistant for a specific topic using a system prompt. Example: a "homework helper" that only assists with science questions, always shows working, and never just gives the answer.
Track B: Image Classifier App | Build an image classifier using Teachable Machine and embed it in a simple webpage. Example: a "plant health checker" that identifies healthy vs diseased leaves.
Track C: AI Story Generator | Build an interactive choose-your-own-adventure story powered by AI. The reader's choices update the AI prompt, generating new story branches.
Track D: AI Research Assistant | Design a prompt system that helps students research a topic responsibly — breaking down a topic, suggesting sources, summarising findings, and flagging potential misinformation.

## Activity 1 — Problem Sprint
📋  Facilitator Instructions
Give each student 10 sticky notes. Set a 5-minute timer. / Task: Write one problem per sticky note — problems from school, home, your community, your interests. No filtering. / Group students into teams of 3. Each team clusters their problems by theme on a large sheet of paper. / Teams pick ONE problem to pursue. It should be: specific, genuinely felt, and potentially solvable with a digital tool.

📝  Problem Definition Template
Complete this statement to define your problem: / WHO has this problem? (Be specific — "Year 7 students who study at home alone" not just "students") / WHAT is the problem? (Describe the situation, not the solution) / WHEN/WHERE does this problem occur? / WHY does it matter? What is the impact if it remains unsolved? / HOW do people currently solve it? What is wrong with the current solution? / Your Problem Statement: "[Person] needs [need] because [insight]." / Example: "Year 7 students studying alone at home need a way to check their own understanding of maths concepts because they have no one to ask and get stuck for hours, losing motivation."
