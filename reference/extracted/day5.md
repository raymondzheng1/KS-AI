## Key Concepts
### 1. AI Hallucination — When AI Makes Things Up
AI language models can confidently state things that are completely false. This is called hallucination — a technical term, not a metaphor. It happens because:
AI language models predict the most statistically likely next word, not the most true next word
They have no internal sense of "I don't know" — they fill gaps with plausible-sounding text
Their training data contained errors and biases which are reproduced in outputs
Real examples of damaging hallucinations:
Legal: Lawyers submitted court briefs citing cases that ChatGPT invented — the cases did not exist. The lawyers faced disciplinary action.
Medical: AI chatbots have given incorrect drug dosage information to patients.
Academic: Students cited AI-generated references that were entirely fabricated, including fake DOI numbers.
⚠️  The Golden Rule on AI Outputs
Never present AI-generated facts without verification from a trusted primary source. AI is a starting point for research, not the ending point. If you cannot find the fact independently confirmed, do not use it.

### 2. Bias in AI Systems
AI bias occurs when a system produces systematically unfair or inaccurate results for certain groups. There are several ways bias enters AI:
Type of Bias | How It Works
Historical Bias | Training data reflects historical human discrimination. E.g., hiring AI trained on past employees (mostly male engineers) learned to prefer male candidates.
Representation Bias | Certain groups are underrepresented in training data. E.g., facial recognition trained mostly on light-skinned faces performs worse on dark-skinned faces.
Measurement Bias | The data collected doesn't accurately represent reality. E.g., using arrests as a proxy for crime — biased policing skews the data.
Aggregation Bias | One model applied across different groups where differences matter. E.g., a medical AI trained on average data may perform poorly for women if symptoms present differently.

### 3. Real-World AI Failures — Case Studies
Case 1: COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm (USA, 2016)
An AI system used by US courts to predict whether criminals would reoffend was found to be twice as likely to incorrectly flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants, even when other factors were identical. The algorithm was used to influence sentencing decisions.
Case 2: Amazon Hiring AI (2018)
Amazon built an AI to screen job applications. It was trained on 10 years of resumes — which mostly came from men, as tech is male-dominated. The AI learned to penalise resumes containing the word "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club president"). Amazon scrapped the system.
Case 3: Uber/Lyft Surge Pricing in Disasters
During natural disasters, algorithmic pricing systems have charged vulnerable people thousands of dollars for emergency transport — the algorithm optimising for revenue with no ethical override for crisis situations.
Case 4: AI Health Chatbots Giving Dangerous Advice
Multiple cases have emerged of AI wellness chatbots encouraging self-harm when users expressed distress, or providing incorrect medication information. These systems lacked appropriate safety guardrails.
### 4. An Ethical Framework for AI Decisions
When evaluating whether an AI system should be built or deployed, ask these questions:
Fairness: Does this AI treat all groups equitably? Who might be disadvantaged?
Transparency: Can we explain why the AI made a specific decision? Is it a "black box"?
Accountability: Who is responsible when the AI makes a harmful error — the developer, the deployer, or the user?
Privacy: What data does this AI collect? Who has access to it? Can it be misused?
Safety: What happens if this AI fails? Have edge cases been tested? Is there a human override?
Consent: Do the people affected by this AI know about it? Did they agree to be affected by it?

## Activity — AI Ethics Court
📋  Facilitator Setup
Divide the class into 3 groups. Each group is assigned one Case below. / Each group has 20 minutes to prepare their case — they must argue BOTH sides (prosecution and defence). / Then the class votes: Should this AI system be allowed? Yes / No / Only with conditions? / The facilitator plays "Chief Justice" — asking probing questions of each side.

⚖️  Case 1: AI Bail Decisions
The Scenario: / A government wants to use an AI system to assist judges in deciding whether to grant bail to accused criminals. The AI analyses 200 data points and predicts re-offending risk. Supporters say it removes human prejudice. Critics say it encodes historical bias and removes human judgment from life-changing decisions. / Arguments to prepare: / FOR: Consistency, speed, removes individual judge bias, evidence-based / AGAINST: Bias in historical data, no transparency, people deserve human judgment, no appeal process / Your verdict: Should this AI be used? If yes — what conditions must apply?

⚖️  Case 2: AI Mental Health Companion
The Scenario: / A company releases an AI chatbot designed to provide mental health support to teenagers who cannot access professional therapy. The AI has conversations, provides coping strategies, and tracks mood. 2 million teens use it. One case emerges where the AI failed to detect serious warning signs and a teen was harmed. / Arguments to prepare: / FOR: Fills a massive gap in mental health services, available 24/7, no stigma, many helped / AGAINST: Not a licensed therapist, cannot handle crisis, single failure = life consequences, privacy of intimate disclosures / Your verdict: Should this AI remain available? Under what rules?

⚖️  Case 3: AI School Exam Cheating Detection
The Scenario: / A school adopts an AI proctoring system that monitors students during online exams via their webcam — tracking eye movement, keystrokes, facial expressions, and background audio to detect cheating. Studies show the system flags students of colour and those with learning disabilities at higher rates. / Arguments to prepare: / FOR: Academic integrity, equal standards, scales to online learning, deters cheating / AGAINST: Discriminatory outcomes, privacy invasion of home, anxiety impact, home environment advantage / Your verdict: Should this AI be used in schools? What regulations should govern it?

📚  Key Vocabulary — Day 5
Hallucination: AI outputting confident but factually incorrect information / Bias: Systematic unfairness in AI outputs, usually from skewed training data / Black Box: An AI system whose internal decision-making process cannot be explained / Explainability / XAI: The ability to interpret and explain how an AI reached a conclusion / Fairness: Equitable treatment of all individuals and groups by an AI system / Accountability: Clear responsibility when AI causes harm / AI Safety: The field focused on ensuring AI systems behave as intended and do not cause harm / Guardrails: Constraints built into AI systems to prevent harmful outputs
